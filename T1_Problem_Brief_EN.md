**Problem Title:** Strategic Integration of Artificial Intelligence in Scientific Research at EGADE Business School

---

## Context and Background

EGADE Business School has faced growing pressure over the past 1–2 years to integrate artificial intelligence into its scientific research processes. The rise of generative AI tools (2023–2024) accelerated internal demand: faculty researchers began exploring AI individually and in an uncoordinated manner, without institutional guidelines, systematic training, or clear policies on the ethical and methodological boundaries of its application. More recently, the emergence of agentic AI — systems capable of autonomously planning, executing multi-step tasks, and operating with minimal human oversight — has added a new layer of complexity, as its potential impact on research design, data analysis, and knowledge synthesis is still poorly understood within the academic community.

At the same time, the external environment is shifting: scientific journals and academic publishers are updating their policies on AI use in research production, placing EGADE researchers in a position of uncertainty — what can be used, how must it be disclosed, and what constitutes assisted authorship vs. delegated authorship? This ambiguity slows rigorous adoption and exposes the institution to academic integrity risks.

The Dean of Scientific Research acknowledges the problem, but the institution has yet to translate that recognition into a roadmap, a governance structure, or dedicated resources. The result is a state of intermediate adoption: there is genuine interest, but no structure to channel it.

---

## Observable Symptoms

- Researchers use AI in an isolated and uncoordinated way, without shared methodological criteria, generating inconsistency in the quality and rigor of scientific output.
- There is no institutional policy on the ethical and transparent use of AI in research (disclosure requirements, authorship boundaries, validation of AI-generated results).
- Scientific journals and publishers are issuing new AI policies; EGADE researchers have no institutional guidance to comply with them.
- There is no formal training program on rigorous AI use — including generative and agentic AI — for scientific research within the Business School.
- The topic has no clear institutional owner and no dedicated budget within the academic structure.

---

## Strategic Importance

This problem matters now because the window for institutional positioning is closing. Business schools that define a clear AI integration strategy for research today will compound advantages over time: higher publication output, stronger methodological rigor, preferential access to international funding and research networks, and greater ability to attract research talent. Those that do not will fall behind in rankings, accreditations, and academic relevance within 12–18 months.

Scientific production is a business school's primary long-term reputational asset. If competitors adopt AI systematically — including agentic AI for literature reviews, hypothesis generation, and data synthesis — and EGADE does not, the gap in output volume and publication quality will widen progressively. Furthermore, the Dean of Scientific Research holds both the authority and the mandate to act, making this the right moment for a strategic intervention.

---

## What Makes This Hard

The problem has two structural complexities that make it difficult to solve with conventional approaches. First, AI — and particularly agentic AI — evolves faster than any institutional policy: what is designed today may be obsolete within months, creating resistance to committing to a roadmap. Second, there is no consensus in the academic community on what constitutes ethical use of AI in scientific research — the boundaries between legitimate assistance and inappropriate delegation of authorship are genuinely ambiguous, and each discipline within EGADE (finance, marketing, strategy, operations) has distinct needs and risks. Solving this requires not only a policy, but a faculty-driven consensus-building process that is robust enough to remain relevant as the technology continues to evolve.
